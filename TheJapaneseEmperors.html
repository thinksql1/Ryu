The Japanese Emperors

Evolution of Imperial Legitimation

From Son of Heaven to Cakravartin

The Japanese emperors are central to understanding the symbiotic relationship between Buddhism and the state. Their role underwent a profound evolution, transitioning from a basis of Confucian and Shinto legitimation to a deep Buddhistization of their authority. This transformation redefined their spiritual and political power, recasting them in the image of the ideal Buddhist monarch, the cakravartin.

Standard Academic Dimensions

Historical Context

▶
  • Emperors existed within the ritsuryō state.
  • They were traditionally legitimized as the "Son of Heaven" (Confucian) and as descendants of the sun goddess Amaterasu (Shinto).
  • Over time, their role became increasingly intertwined with Buddhist rituals and ideology.

Textual Role: The Coronation Abhiṣeka

▶

The emperors are protagonists in the transformation of imperial legitimation. Their narrative arc involves adopting Buddhist practices, most notably the coronation abhiṣeka. This ritual transformed them into a cakravartin.

They moved from being the "pivot of the rectification of names" in Confucianism to a "sign generated from the intertwining of diverse symbols of royalty in the Buddhist scriptures".

Details of the Abhiṣeka:

  • Part 1: Prior transmission of secret mantras and mudras from an Esoteric Buddhist master.
  • Part 2: Repeating the mudra sequence upon being crowned.
  • The five mudras of the five eyes were central: human, heavenly, wisdom, Dharma, and Buddha's eye, which resembled the wish-fulfilling jewel, or cintāmaṇi.

Moral and Ethical Themes

▶
  • Emperors were expected to embody virtues of a cakravartin, particularly mastery of the ten good deeds (prohibitions against killing, stealing, improper sexual acts; lying, flattery, slander, duplicity; greed, rage, and folly).
  • They served as exemplary lay Buddhist patrons of the Sangha, hosting grand Buddhist ceremonies like the Mishuhō and the lecture on the Sutra of the Virtuous King.

Linguistic Analysis: Reinterpreting the Regalia

▶

The imperial regalia (gem, sword, mirror) were reinterpreted through Buddhist symbolism to legitimize imperial power. This demonstrates "the Buddhist refiguration of the emperor's authority."

  • Gem (Yasakani no magatama): Identified with Buddhalocanā and the empress, symbolizing the Dharma and the jewel queen (cintāmaṇi).
  • Sword (Ama no murakumo no tsurugi): Identified with Ekākṣara-uṣṇīṣacakra and the emperor, symbolizing the ruler's power that must be controlled by the Dharma.
  • Mirror (Yata no kagami): Identified with Amaterasu and Mahāvairocana, and the crown prince, symbolizing the Son of Heaven.

Comparative Analysis

▶
  • The concept of cakravartin linked Japanese emperors to a broader Buddhist ideal of universal rulership.
  • The identification of Amaterasu with Mahāvairocana provided a crucial bridge between Shinto and Buddhist beliefs.
  • Emperor Shōmu's dream, where Amaterasu revealed her identity as Mahāvairocana, served as a foundational mythological claim for Tōdaiji's religious suzerainty and the oneness of Shinto, Esoteric, and Exoteric Buddhism.

Think-Outside-the-Box Angles

Hemispheric Framing

Left Hemisphere: The elaborate, standardized procedures of the coronation ceremony, state administration, and detailed laws reflect logical, administrative functions.

Right Hemisphere: The symbolic transformation into a cakravartin, receiving secret mantras and mudras, and the spiritual understanding of their role point to intuitive and embodied knowing.

Energetic/Archetypal Mapping

Emperors represent the King archetype, evolving to embody the Cakravartin (universal Dharma King) and the Son of Heaven. Specific divinities like Buddhalocanā (gem/empress) and Ekākṣara-uṣṇīṣacakra (sword/emperor) are directly mapped onto the imperial figures and regalia.

Dream Analysis Style

Emperor Shōmu's dream serves as a significant example of dreams as a medium for spiritual and political legitimation in this context, directly linking the sun goddess Amaterasu to the cosmic Buddha Mahāvairocana.

Symbolic Synesthesia

Sensory Evocation: The emperors' presence would evoke gold (Mahāvairocana, splendor) and incense (ritual sanctity).

Imagine: A room designed around an emperor would convey weighty authority, intricate regalia, and sacred reverence.

Relational Web

Emperors were influenced by powerful clerics like Kūkai and Jien, who helped shape their Buddhist identity. They, in turn, patronized monasteries and clergy, fostering a complex web of mutual dependence. The Sangha legitimized the emperor as a cakravartin, while the emperor affirmed the authenticity of the Eight Schools of Buddhist disciplines as the religious orthodoxy of the state.
